# The Ground Game — What Happens Before the Automation

This is the part of GTM that does not scale, cannot be automated, and determines whether everything else works.

The 15 workflows documented in the [automation architecture](automation-architecture.md) are the destination. This document is the road you walk to get there. No automation replaces a founding GTM lead getting their hands dirty in the first weeks. You need to understand the product, talk to real people, build relationships, and earn the right to automate.

---

## The Reality of Week 0

You have just been hired as the founding GTM lead. There is no pipeline. No brand awareness. No outbound infrastructure. No playbook. Just a product, a founder, and an existing customer base that most people would overlook.

The instinct is to start building workflows and buying tools. Resist it.

Before you touch a single automation, you need to do three things: learn the product deeply enough to demo it in your sleep, talk to enough people to understand how they describe the problem in their own words, and figure out who actually has the authority and urgency to buy. Everything else — the email sequences, the scoring models, the signal intelligence — comes after.

---

## Get in the Mud

### Learn the Product by Using It

Before you write a single email, use the product yourself. Sit in on demos. Read the documentation. Watch a referral flow through the system from start to finish. Understand how an AI link worker actually handles a patient, what data it needs, what it produces, where it fits in a GP surgery's existing workflow.

If you cannot explain what the product does in one sentence to an NHS Clinical Director, you are not ready to send outbound.

One sentence. Not a paragraph. Not a feature list. One sentence that makes them lean forward.

### Talk to Real People First

Set up 5-10 conversations in the first week. Not pitches. Conversations. Call existing customers. Call prospects. Call people who said no. Ask what their biggest pain is. Listen to how they describe the problem — their words, not yours.

The language they use becomes the language you write in. When a Clinical Director says "we can't keep link workers long enough to train them properly," that exact phrase is worth more than anything you will write at your desk. This is what the [email methodology](copy-framework.md) means by peer-to-peer tone — it comes from actually understanding the peer's world.

### Define Your ICP Through Conversations, Not Spreadsheets

The ICP in [strategy.md](strategy.md) is a hypothesis. Conversations validate it. After 10 calls, you will know which tier actually has budget authority, which titles are decision-makers versus influencers, and which pain points make people lean forward versus politely nod.

Update the [scoring criteria](scoring-criteria.md) based on what you learn. If every conversation tells you PCN Clinical Directors have the pain but not the budget, and ICB Directors of Primary Care have both — that changes your entire outreach priority.

The ICP is not a document you write once. It is a living model that sharpens every week based on what the market tells you.

### Set Up Your Channels

Week 1 checklist — stand these up early because they all take time to compound:

- **LinkedIn**: Optimise your profile for the market. Not a CV — a "here's how I can help" profile. Start connecting with ICP titles. Join relevant NHS digital health groups.
- **Email infrastructure**: Purchase domains and start warmup immediately. This takes 2-3 weeks before you can send a single cold email. Every day you wait is a day you cannot get back. See the volume ramp in [strategy.md](strategy.md) for targets.
- **WhatsApp**: Join 2-3 NHS social prescribing communities. Listen before you post. Understand what people talk about and what they care about.
- **CRM**: Set up pipeline stages that match the NHS buying cycle. These are long cycles with multiple stakeholders — your pipeline needs to reflect that reality.
- **Scheduling**: Set up Calendly or equivalent. Remove every possible friction point between "I'm interested" and "we're having a conversation."

Every channel you set up in Week 1 compounds. Domain warmup takes 2-3 weeks. LinkedIn connections take time to accept. WhatsApp trust takes time to build. If you wait until Week 3 to start, you are already behind.

## Work the Warm List First

### The Unfair Advantage

The parent company has 1,200+ GP surgeries in its network, 18 million patients, and roughly 15% GP market share. Over a million patients have been referred into an ecosystem of 30,000 local services. When patients attend these services, they visit the GP surgery 39% less than before, use A&E 26% less, and have 41% fewer fit notes.

That is not just a stat for a pitch deck. It is a database of warm introductions sitting right in front of you. Every cold email you do not have to send is an inbox you do not burn.

### How to Map the Existing Network

Step by step:

1. Get the full customer list from the parent company
2. Map which ICBs those GP surgeries sit within
3. Identify the 10-15 ICBs with the highest existing GP coverage — these are your warmest territories
4. Find the decision-makers at those ICBs (Director of Primary Care, Head of Social Prescribing — reference the Tier 1 titles in [strategy.md](strategy.md))
5. Ask existing GP contacts for warm introductions upward — "Your practices already use our platform. We've built something new. Can you connect me with your ICB lead?"

This is the fastest path to first demos. You are not starting from zero. You are starting from a position of existing trust.

### The Warm Outreach Sequence

This is not cold outbound copy — that is in the [copy framework](copy-framework.md). This is warm outreach where trust already exists through the parent company relationship.

**Email example:**

```
Subject: quick question

Hi [Name],

Several GP practices in [ICB name] already use our platform for social prescribing referrals
— [Practice A] and [Practice B] among them.

We've built an AI link worker that sits on top of the existing system. Early data shows GP
visits drop 39% and A&E usage drops 26% when patients are actively supported.

Would it be worth 15 minutes to show you what it looks like in practice?

[Your name]
```

**LinkedIn DM example:**

```
Hi [Name] — I work with several GP practices in your ICB area on social prescribing.
We've just launched an AI link worker and I'd love to get your take on whether it fits
what you're seeing on the ground. Worth a quick chat?
```

**WhatsApp follow-up (after initial contact):**

```
Hi [Name], just following up on my email last week about the AI link worker.
Happy to jump on a quick call whenever works — no pressure either way.
```

### Why This Matters Before Cold

Warm conversations teach you the market. The objections you hear in warm calls become the objections you preempt in cold copy. The language they use becomes the language you write. And the proof points from early warm demos become the credibility you need for cold outbound to work.

| Approach | Conversion to Demo | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Warm intro from existing GP contact | 40-60% | Trust already exists through the parent company |
| Cold email to same ICB (with context) | 5-8% | No existing relationship, but relevant territory |
| Cold email to unknown ICB | 2-4% | Starting completely from zero |

Start warm. Learn fast. Then go cold with real ammunition.

## Cold Email Infrastructure — The Part Nobody Warns You About

### Starting Is Easy

Getting cold email up and running is not that hard anymore. You have tools for copy, tools to map your TAM and pull data, tools like Instantly to manage deliverability. Buy some domains, set up warmup, write sequences, hit send. There are 50 YouTube tutorials on this. The first 2-3 weeks feel great — open rates are high, replies trickle in, you think you have cracked it.

If you are a founder with more time than budget, there is no harm in giving it a crack yourself. Starting is easy. Scaling is where people get wrecked.

### The Week 4 Wall

Here is what actually happens. You launch, you see results, you get excited. Around week 4, your inboxes start to degrade. You panic. You start testing new copy, new offers, new subject lines. Over the next couple of weeks, replies basically go to zero.

You think: "I guess cold email doesn't work."

What actually happened: your inboxes are cooked.

Reply rates can decrease up to 4x in a couple of weeks — same copy, same data, same offer. The only thing that changed was inbox health. If your offer is not performing in the first place, inboxes degrade faster than 4 weeks and no amount of rotation saves you. That is a different problem.

### Inbox-Level Monitoring (Not Campaign-Level)

Your campaign metrics can look fine while your inboxes are quietly dying.

This is the key insight most people miss. Campaign-level metrics — aggregate open rate, aggregate reply rate — hide the real problem. One inbox might be landing in spam while another is fine. The campaign average looks "OK" but half your emails are going nowhere.

I had a campaign performing well, then results started sliding. A couple tenths of a percent each day. Nothing dramatic enough to panic over, but trending the wrong direction.

Was not a data problem. Was not a copy problem.

Built a quick automation off the Instantly API to pull inbox-level performance data. Broke it down by batch — we run A/B inbox rotations on a 3-4 week cycle — and looked at week-over-week trends.

What I found: during the rotation handoff, the batches got swapped. The fatigued inboxes started ramping back up instead of down. Fresh inboxes got pulled back. Fixed it same day. Results came back, actually better than before.

The point: if you are only looking at campaign-level reply rates, you will miss this every time. The slide is so gradual you will convince yourself it is a down week until you are 4 weeks deep with fried inboxes.

**What to monitor per inbox:**

- Individual open rate (floor: 30%. Below this, pull the inbox immediately)
- Individual reply rate
- Bounce rate per inbox
- Warmup health score
- Spam placement rate (if your tool provides it)

### The Rotation System

The setup that keeps your infrastructure alive:

1. Buy twice as many domains and inboxes as you think you need
2. Label them **Set A** and **Set B**
3. Set A is active for 3-4 weeks while Set B warms up (or rests)
4. Beginning of week 4, gradually ramp Set A down and Set B up
5. Repeat every 3-4 weeks

By the time Set A would have started degrading, Set B is already warmed and taking over. Set A gets a rest cycle to recover its reputation.

The even better version: do this at the individual inbox level. Monitor performance and swap individual inboxes as soon as they start to slide. But if you are managing this yourself, the A/B rotation is a simple system that mostly guarantees you maintain deliverability.

### Domain and Inbox Math

Go conservative on infrastructure. More inboxes at lower volume is always better than fewer inboxes at higher volume.

| Scale | Active Domains | Inboxes per Domain | Sends per Inbox/Day | Total Daily Volume |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Weeks 3-4 (start) | 2-3 | 2 | 10-15 | 40-90 |
| Month 2 | 4-5 | 2 | 10-15 | 80-150 |
| Month 3 | 6-8 | 2 | 10-15 | 120-240 |

These are **active sending** inboxes. You need the same number resting or warming. So double the domain count for your total inventory.

These targets align with the volume ramp in [strategy.md](strategy.md): 10-15/day in Weeks 3-4, 25-40 in Month 2, 50-75 in Month 3. The infrastructure has to exist before the volume can.

### When Your Offer Is the Problem

No amount of inbox rotation saves a bad offer. If your reply rate is below 2% after testing 4-5 angles across clean infrastructure, the market is telling you something. Go back to Section 1. Talk to more people. Refine the offer.

The [copy framework](copy-framework.md) gives you the methodology for writing emails that get replies. But the offer itself has to resonate. Infrastructure is a necessary condition, not a sufficient one.

Killing a campaign angle is not a failure. It is the system working. Every angle that does not land teaches you something about the market — what language resonates, what pain points are real, what timing matters.

## The Scaling Reality

### The Transition From Manual to Managed

In Week 1, you are doing everything by hand. Checking NHS Jobs yourself. Reading board papers yourself. Sending LinkedIn messages one at a time. Manually tracking who replied, who objected, who went quiet.

By Month 2, you should be transitioning these repetitive tasks to the automation suite documented in the [automation architecture](automation-architecture.md). The ground game does not stop — it evolves. You move from "setting up meetings myself" to "managing the system that sets up meetings."

### When Infrastructure Becomes a Full-Time Job

At 50+ emails per day across 6-8 active inboxes, monitoring and rotating infrastructure takes 1-2 hours daily. At 100+, it is closer to half a day. This is the point where you decide: do I want to spend my time managing inboxes or closing deals?

If you have the time and want to keep costs low, managing infrastructure yourself is viable at lower volumes. But if you want hundreds or thousands of leads a month, the infrastructure management, CRM integration, data pipelines, and deliverability monitoring turns into a full-time job fast.

| Signal | Action |
|---|---|
| Sending fewer than 50/day, fewer than 5 active inboxes | Manage yourself |
| Sending 50-100/day, reply rate healthy | Consider a VA for inbox monitoring |
| Sending 100+/day, rotation cycles every 3 weeks | Dedicated infrastructure person or outsource |
| Reply rate consistently below 2% despite clean infrastructure | Stop scaling. Fix the offer. |

### What the Automation Suite Actually Replaces

The key principle: you have to do these things manually first to understand what the automation needs to do. Building a workflow before you have done the task yourself means you do not know what a good signal looks like. The ground game is not the opposite of automation — it is the prerequisite.

| Manual Activity (Ground Game) | Automated Workflow | What It Does |
|---|---|---|
| Checking NHS Jobs for link worker vacancies | WF-01a/b | Scrapes NHS Jobs weekly, enriches leads via Clay |
| Reading ICB board papers for buying signals | WF-04 | AI reads board papers, scores urgency, routes signals |
| Manually tracking LinkedIn engagement | WF-02a/b, WF-06a/b | Captures engagement in real-time and batch, routes by ICP |
| Classifying email replies and deciding next action | WF-03 | AI classifies every reply into 6 categories, takes action |
| Monitoring competitor contracts for renewal windows | WF-07 | Watches procurement portals for contracts expiring within 90 days |
| Tracking NHS leadership changes | WF-08 | Detects new appointments, checks for warm intro paths |
| Pilot check-ins, milestone tracking, case studies | WF-05a-d | Automates full pilot lifecycle from onboarding to case study |

## Verification Checkpoints

### End of Week 1

- [ ] Have you used the product yourself? Can you demo it without notes?
- [ ] Have you had 5+ real conversations with prospects or existing customers?
- [ ] Are domains purchased and warming?
- [ ] Is the parent company's customer list mapped to ICBs?
- [ ] Are LinkedIn, WhatsApp, and CRM channels set up?
- [ ] Do you have a one-sentence explanation of the product that makes people lean forward?

### End of Week 2

- [ ] Have you initiated 5-8 warm introductions through existing GP contacts?
- [ ] Have you booked at least 2 demo conversations?
- [ ] Can you articulate the top 3 objections you have heard — in the prospect's own words?
- [ ] Is your first cold email sequence drafted (not sent — infrastructure is still warming)?
- [ ] Have you updated the ICP scoring based on what conversations have taught you?

### End of Week 4

- [ ] First cold campaigns live? Open rates above 45%?
- [ ] Are you monitoring inbox-level metrics, not just campaign-level?
- [ ] Have you killed at least one underperforming campaign angle?
- [ ] Is your inbox rotation plan documented and scheduled?
- [ ] Have you had at least one warm demo that generated real feedback on the offer?
