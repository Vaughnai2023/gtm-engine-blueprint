# Email Copy Methodology

Cold email is a craft. Most outbound fails because it reads like marketing — long, formal, and self-centred. This document explains the methodology I use to write emails that actually get replies.

---

## Brevity Rules

Every email is 70-90 words. That is about 4-5 sentences. Short enough to read on a phone in 10 seconds. If you can't say it in 90 words, you are trying to say too much. Cut it in half. Then cut it again.

---

## Tone

Peer-to-peer. Write like you are texting a colleague, not pitching a product. No corporate language. No "I hope this email finds you well." No "I'd love to schedule a brief call to discuss synergies." Just talk like a normal person.

The reader should feel like this came from someone who understands their world. Not from a marketing department.

This is not just a tactic — it is a value. NHS professionals are stretched thin. Their inboxes are full of vendor noise. Respecting their time with short, honest, relevant messages is the right thing to do. That it also converts better is a bonus, not the reason.

---

## Subject Lines

Three to five words, all lowercase, no punctuation. The goal is to look like an internal email. Something like "quick question about hiring" beats "Innovative AI Solution for Your Link Worker Challenges" every time.

Lowercase signals casual. Short signals relevant. No punctuation signals real. These three things together get the email opened.

---

## CTAs (Calls to Action)

Soft asks only. "Worth a look?" or "Interested?" Never "Book a 30-minute demo" or "Let's schedule a call."

Lower the barrier to reply. A one-word response is still a response. Once someone replies, you are in a conversation. That is the whole point.

---

## Format

Plain text only. No HTML, no images, no tracked links. This is not about aesthetics. It is about deliverability.

Plain text emails land in the primary inbox. HTML emails with tracking pixels get flagged by spam filters. Every link you add, every image you embed, every pixel you track makes it more likely your email ends up in a folder no one checks.

---

## One Job Per Email

Each email makes one point and one ask. Don't cram a case study, a product overview, and a meeting request into a single message. Make your point. Ask your question. Stop.

If you have three things to say, that is three emails. Not one long one.

---

## Sequence Design

Emails are grouped into sequences of 2-4 steps. Each step has a specific job.

- **Step 1:** Introduce. State the problem. Ask if it resonates.
- **Step 2:** Add proof. A number, a result, a relevant example.
- **Step 3:** Try a new angle. Approach the same problem from a different direction.
- **Step 4:** Break up gracefully. "I'll assume the timing isn't right."

Timing matters. Space emails 3-4 days apart. Enough time that you are not annoying. Close enough that you are still relevant.

The breakup email is important. It is respectful. It creates urgency. And it often gets the best reply rate of the whole sequence.

---

## Personalisation

Every email references something specific to the recipient. Their company. A hiring signal. A board paper mention. A LinkedIn post they engaged with.

Generic does not count. "I noticed your company is growing" is lazy. "Saw you've had 3 link worker roles open for 6 weeks" is specific. The difference between those two sentences is the difference between delete and reply.

---

## Campaign Angles

Run 4-5 different angles at the same time. Each angle approaches the same problem from a different direction.

- Workforce gap angle.
- Cost pressure angle.
- Outcomes and impact angle.
- Compliance and mandate angle.
- Patient experience angle.

Kill underperformers weekly. Double down on winners. The copy framework is a living document that evolves based on what works. What gets replies this month might not work next month. Test constantly.

Killing a campaign is not a failure. It is the system working. Every angle that does not land teaches you something about the market — what language resonates, what pain points are real, what timing matters. The graft of writing, testing, and iterating is where the real learning happens.

---

## How AI Applies This

The copy framework is a strategy document that the AI orchestration layer reads when generating sequences. When I say "write a 3-email sequence using the workforce gap angle," the AI follows these rules exactly — word count, tone, CTA style, subject line format.

The result reads like it was written by a human who understands the recipient's world. And it takes seconds instead of an hour.
